# The Revolt of 1857

The Revolt of 1857, also known as India's First War of Independence or the Sepoy Mutiny, was a major uprising against the rule of the British East India Company. It began on 10 May 1857 in the town of Meerut and quickly spread across northern and central India, shaking the foundations of British power on the subcontinent.

Although the revolt was ultimately suppressed, it marked a turning point in Indian history. It ended the rule of the East India Company and forced the British Crown to take direct control of India in 1858.

## Causes of the Revolt

The uprising was the result of decades of resentment against British policies. These grievances built up across political, economic, military, social, and religious lines.

### Political Causes

The British policy of expansion angered many Indian rulers. The **Doctrine of Lapse**, introduced by Lord Dalhousie, allowed the Company to annex any princely state whose ruler died without a natural male heir. States such as Jhansi, Satara, and Nagpur were absorbed this way, creating deep anger among the displaced royalty.

### Economic Causes

Heavy taxation and the destruction of traditional Indian industries left peasants and artisans impoverished. British revenue policies forced many farmers into debt, while cheap machine-made goods from Britain ruined local craftsmen.

### Military Causes

Indian soldiers, known as **sepoys**, formed the backbone of the Company's army but were paid less and treated as inferior to British soldiers. The immediate trigger was the introduction of the new **Enfield rifle**, whose cartridges were rumoured to be greased with cow and pig fat — offending both Hindu and Muslim soldiers.

## Consequences of the Revolt

Though the revolt failed to achieve its goal of ending British rule, its consequences were far-reaching and permanent.

- The **East India Company was abolished**, and India came under the direct rule of the British Crown.
- The **Government of India Act 1858** transferred power to the Crown, with a Secretary of State for India appointed in Britain.
- The British reorganised the army and adopted a policy of **divide and rule** to prevent future uprisings.
- The last Mughal emperor, Bahadur Shah Zafar, was exiled to Rangoon, formally ending the Mughal dynasty.

## Why It Matters

While the rebellion was militarily unsuccessful, it laid the foundation for the modern Indian national movement. It shattered the myth of British invincibility and created a shared sense of unity among diverse groups who fought against a common power. For this reason, many historians regard 1857 as the **first war of Indian independence**.
